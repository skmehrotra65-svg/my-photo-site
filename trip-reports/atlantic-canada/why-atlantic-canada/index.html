Field note 01 · Before departure

Why Atlantic Canada—
and why now

A journey that began with photography and became something larger: an opportunity to travel deeply, experience a remarkable part of Canada and build a chapter of our family history.

Words by Sanjay MehrotraJuly 2026 · Coquitlam, Canada

For years, Atlantic Canada has occupied a special place in my imagination—a landscape of rugged coastlines, fishing villages, shifting fog and wildlife shaped by the cold North Atlantic. It feels both unmistakably Canadian and entirely different from the Canada I know on the Pacific coast.

This journey began with photography. I wanted to photograph puffins gathered along sea cliffs, whales moving through open water, the extraordinary tides of the Bay of Fundy and—if patience and luck cooperate—a moose emerging in the soft light of early morning.

But photography is only part of the reason we are going.

“I want to experience that culture with my family, not merely pass through it in search of photographs.”

What makes Atlantic Canada compelling is the relationship between the landscape and the people who live there. Newfoundland music, historic outports, working harbours, Acadian influences and small communities shaped by the sea all give the region a human story.

Our route reflects the way we prefer to travel. I will often head out alone before sunrise, when wildlife is active and the landscape is quiet. Later in the morning, I will rejoin my wife and son for the shared part of the day—boat trips, coastal towns, local performances, scenic drives and unhurried meals.

Why now?

We have deliberately avoided turning the journey into a race. The itinerary has changed many times as we balanced ambition with the realities of family travel: driving distances, ferry logistics, changing hotels and the fatigue that comes from trying to see everything. The final route uses two separate coastal loops, connected by air, allowing us to experience Newfoundland and the Maritimes without forcing every possible destination into one trip.

Perhaps travel feels different at this stage of life. I am increasingly interested in depth rather than quantity—in staying long enough to notice the weather change, returning to a location when the light improves and allowing unexpected encounters to reshape the plan.

My son is fourteen. The years in which the three of us can take extended family journeys together are no longer unlimited. He may not share every one of my dawn photography ambitions, but the experiences we have together—the whales, music, coastlines, conversations and inevitable misadventures—will become part of our family history.

This is therefore more than a sightseeing trip and more than a photography expedition. It is an attempt to be present: to photograph wild places without reducing them to images, to meet people with curiosity and to experience a remarkable part of Canada together.

The plans are ready. The photographs have not yet been made. The real story begins when we reach the Atlantic.
